# Cat vs Dog Routine Differences

Cats and dogs have fundamentally different care routines, and treating them the same way causes problems. Dogs are social pack animals that depend on their owners for most daily activity; cats are semi-solitary hunters that self-regulate much of their behavior but have their own non-negotiable needs.

## Time Commitment

Dogs generally require more active owner time per day than cats. A typical adult dog needs 60 to 120 minutes of direct engagement (walks, training, play, feeding). A typical adult cat needs 30 to 45 minutes (play, feeding, litter box, brushing). Multi-cat households add litter box time but rarely add much play time, since cats often play with each other.

## Walking

Dogs need daily walks for both exercise and bathroom needs. Missing a walk is not optional — it causes behavioral problems, accidents indoors, and stress. Most cats do not walk on leashes and do not need outdoor exercise; indoor play substitutes.

## Litter Box (Cats Only)

Litter boxes need to be scooped **at least once per day**, ideally twice. A full clean with fresh litter is needed every 1 to 2 weeks. The rule of thumb is one box per cat plus one extra. Scooping takes 2 to 5 minutes per day. Cats will refuse to use dirty boxes and will develop elimination problems if boxes are neglected.

## Social Needs

Dogs are typically distressed by being alone for more than 6 to 8 hours. Puppies and senior dogs have much shorter tolerances (2 to 4 hours). Cats generally tolerate solitude better — healthy adult cats can be left for 8 to 12 hours with appropriate food and water, though they still benefit from daily interaction.

## Training

Dogs require active training, especially in the first year. Basic obedience, leash training, and socialization take 10 to 20 minutes per day for several months. Cats can be trained (litter, carrier, basic tricks) but the time investment is typically much smaller.

## Key Takeaway

A schedule built for a dog will overschedule a cat; a schedule built for a cat will dangerously underschedule a dog. Species is one of the most important inputs to any care plan.
